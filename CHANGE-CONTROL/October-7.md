# October 7 Negotiation Cost Study Intake

## Purpose

Frame a policy-analysis study that compares negotiation-first and force-first response scenarios using public, attributable inputs. The output should be an auditable decision-support package, not an advocacy memo.

## Core Question

Using public data, what direct, indirect, legal, diplomatic, and humanitarian costs can be attributed to post-October 7 response pathways, and how do those costs compare against negotiation-based counterfactual scenarios?

## Study Scope

- Time horizon: October 7, 2023 onward.
- Actors in scope: Israel, Hamas, the United States, Iran, regional proxies, and affected civilians.
- Cost classes: lives lost, injuries, displacement, infrastructure loss, military spending, hostage outcomes, legal exposure, diplomatic fallout, and longer-tail regional effects.
- Scenario bands: best-case, base-case, and worst-case negotiation assumptions compared with force-dominant alternatives.

## Research Inputs

- Public casualty and hostage reporting with provenance.
- Public budget, aid, military, reconstruction, and insurance estimates.
- Legal and diplomatic sources covering sanctions, court actions, and treaty implications.
- Explicit assumptions for missing or disputed values.

## Expected Outputs

- Transparent scenario model with low/base/high ranges.
- Cost-per-life-saved sensitivity table for illustrative ranges such as $1B, $10B, and $100B.
- Option comparison that separates descriptive analysis from normative judgment.
- Traceability from assumptions to outputs.

## Guardrails

- Use only public or user-supplied sources.
- Mark disputed figures and confidence levels explicitly.
- Avoid operational, tactical, or coercive guidance.
- Keep humanitarian, legal, and policy framing distinct.
